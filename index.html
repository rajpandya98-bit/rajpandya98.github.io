Featured Project: Fall Detection System

This project focuses on the design and evaluation of a fall detection system intended to demonstrate reliable motion sensing and validation-oriented engineering practices commonly used in medical device development. The system was built using a three-axis analog accelerometer (ADXL335) combined with LM358-based signal conditioning to ensure stable and interpretable sensor data.

Rather than prioritizing complex algorithms, the project emphasizes core engineering principles including analog signal integrity, noise reduction, threshold-based event detection, and systematic testing. The system continuously monitors acceleration across multiple axes and identifies fall-like events based on sudden changes exceeding defined limits.

Significant attention was given to verification and validation activities, including bench testing, simulated real-world motion scenarios, and evaluation of false-positive and false-negative behavior. Observed limitations were documented to support a risk-aware and iterative design approach aligned with quality and validation expectations in regulated medical device and pharmaceutical environments.

Quality Engineering Project: Design Verification, Validation & Risk Analysis

Applied quality engineering principles to the fall detection system to evaluate design robustness, safety considerations, and compliance with medical device development practices.

Objective

To verify that design outputs met defined design inputs and to validate that the system performance aligned with intended use, while identifying and mitigating potential risks.

Verification Activities

  • Defined functional design inputs including response time, detection threshold, and signal stability
  • Conducted bench-level verification testing across X, Y, and Z acceleration axes
  • Verified repeatability of sensor response under controlled motion conditions

Validation Activities

  • Simulated real-world use scenarios such as walking, sitting, and fall-like motion
  • Evaluated system behavior relative to intended use rather than only specifications
  • Documented performance limitations observed during testing

Risk Analysis (ISO 14971 – Conceptual)

  • Identified hazards including false negatives, false positives, and sensor drift
  • Assessed potential impact on user safety and system reliability
  • Implemented basic risk controls such as signal filtering, threshold optimization, and calibration checks

Outcome

This project strengthened understanding of quality-driven development by integrating verification, validation, and risk-based thinking into the engineering workflow rather than treating quality as a post-design activity.
